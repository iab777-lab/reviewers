🌋Pacific Ring of Fire

What is the Pacific Ring of Fire?

The Pacific Ring of Fire, also known as the Circum-Pacific Belt, is a major area in the basin of the Pacific Ocean where a large number of earthquakes and volcanic eruptions occur.

40,000 km (25,000 miles) horseshoe shape
452 volcanoes
75% of world's active & dormant volcanoes

Earthquake Statistics

  • 90% of the world's earthquakes occur in the Ring of Fire
  • 80% of the world's largest earthquakes occur here

The Philippines & the Ring of Fire

The Philippines is situated along a highly seismic area lying along the Pacific Ring of Fire. This geographical location makes the country prone to natural disasters.

Major Philippine Earthquakes

While earthquake disasters are not as frequent as typhoons and flooding, the damage to affected communities is usually massive and devastating. Within a 10-year period, five destructive earthquakes were recorded:

  • 2013 Bohol Earthquake
  • 1990 Luzon Earthquake
  • 1976 Moro Gulf Earthquake

Two Major Tectonic Plates

The Philippines sits at the boundary of two major tectonic plates: the Philippine Sea Plate and the Eurasian Plate. This makes the country highly prone to volcanic eruptions and earthquakes. There are around 300 volcanoes within the Pacific Ring of Fire in the Philippines. Economic damage from earthquakes was estimated to reach ₱2.5 billion.

The Philippines must adopt Disaster Risk Reduction and Management (DRRM) and Climate Change Adaptation (CCA) due to its geological vulnerability.

🌋Active Volcanoes in the Philippines

Mount Pinatubo

Mount Pinatubo erupted in 1991 and was considered a terrestrial eruption of the 20th century. Thousands of people were evacuated to save many lives. Fragmentation flows, ash deposits, and lahars destroyed thousands of houses, farmlands, and water resources.

Mayon Volcano

Known as the world's most perfect volcanic cone because of its symmetrical shape, Mayon Volcano is found in Albay, Bicol. Its last major explosion was on September 23, 1984. Eruption characteristics include pyroclastic flow, lava flow, lahar, and ashfall. Its scope was up to 1.7–16 kilometers eruption column, devastating lowland communities around its vicinity.

Taal Volcano

Taal is a small yet one of the most active volcanoes in the Philippines with reported eruptions over the past years. The most recent activity was on March 26, 2022 — Alert Level 3 (Magmatic Unrest). A phreatomagmatic eruption of the Main Crater generated up to 3,000 tall eruption plumes. DOST-PHIVOLCS strongly recommends that islands near the volcano must evacuate due to volcanic tsunamis.

Canlaon Volcano

The most known active volcano in Central Philippines. The last movement was reported on June 5 – July 3, 2022. PHIVOLCS Director Renato Solidum, Jr. advised the community near the 4-kilometer permanent danger zone to avoid getting near the volcano. Increased activity includes volcanic gas and movements of tectonic plates.

🌊Tsunamis

A tsunami is a series of sea waves commonly caused by earthquakes under the sea. Places near coastal areas facing the Pacific Ocean, Celebes Sea, West Philippine Sea, and Sulu Sea are the first to be affected.

In August 1976, the southwest coast of Mindanao had an earthquake with a magnitude of 8.1, producing nine-meter-high tsunamis.

PHIVOLCS Safety & Preparedness Measures for Tsunamis

  • Do not stay in low-lying coastal areas after a felt earthquake. Move to higher grounds immediately.
  • If unusual sea conditions like rapid lowering of sea level are observed, immediately move toward higher ground.
  • Never go down the beach to watch for a tsunami. When you see the wave, you are too close to escape it.
  • During the retreat of sea level, interesting sights are often revealed. Fishes may be stranded on dry land — do not be attracted to collect them; evacuate instead.

💨Storm Surge

Storm surge is a drastic change in water level with the presence of a storm. It is the abnormal rise of water due to heavy storms, over and above the normal tide. As the tropical cyclone approaches the coast, strong winds strike the water over low-lying areas, which can produce flooding.

Storm surge becomes more threatening when it goes on top of a high tide — it may cause a flood. Big and strong waves are produced by the strong winds.

Notable Storm Surges in the Philippines

  • Super Typhoon Yolanda — Most powerful tropical cyclone in the past 20 years; devastated most parts of the country, especially Leyte and Samar.
  • Tropical Cyclone Thelma (1991) — Raged in the Visayas Region; 5,000–8,000 people died.
  • Typhoon Frank — Devastated Metro Manila and other provinces in Luzon.

🌀Typhoons & PAGASA Warning Signals

The Philippine Public Storm Warning Signal has different levels (Signal 1–Signal 5). Each signal level corresponds to its meteorological condition, wind impact, and precautionary measures needed.

The hazard map shows places prone to floods, tropical cyclones, and storm surges. Low-lying places near the seashore and the middle of each archipelago are most at risk.

PAGASA Modified Tropical Cyclone Warning System (March 2022)

🌀 Tropical Depression (TD) Wind: 61 km/h or less — Wind Signal 1
🌀 Tropical Storm (TS) Wind: 62–88 km/h — Wind Signal 2
🌀 Severe Tropical Storm (STS) Wind: 89–117 km/h — Wind Signal 3
🌀 Typhoon (TY) Wind: 118–184 km/h — Wind Signal 4
🌀 Super Typhoon (STY) Wind: 185 km/h or higher — Wind Signal 5

⚠️Disaster Preparedness

As one of the priority areas of NDRRMC (National Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council), it serves as a national guide for disaster prevention and mitigation, disaster preparedness, disaster response, and disaster rehabilitation and recovery — to make citizens prepared in times of calamity.

Different establishments conduct seminars and earthquake drills among their personnel. During calamities, timing is essential to an orderly, safe, and effective evacuation.

During an Earthquake (NDRRMC Guidelines)

  • Stay calm and look for the exit.
  • Avoid using an elevator.
  • Stay under a sturdy table or furniture to protect from falling debris.
  • As soon as the situation normalizes, go outside and stay far from big buildings, structures, electric wires, or trees.

During Stormy Days

  • It is safe to stay at home.
  • Keep away from broken electric wires.
  • Stay away from floods — dirty water can make you sick.

🏝️Three Major Island Groups

The Philippines is divided into three major island groups: Luzon, Visayas, and Mindanao. Each region offers unique geographical features that greatly influence the ways of living and livelihoods of its inhabitants.

🌾Luzon Regions

Region 1: Ilocos Region

Geography: Rugged coastal strip between the Cordillera Mountains and the West Philippine Sea

Provinces: Ilocos Norte, Ilocos Sur, La Union, Pangasinan

Livelihoods:

  • Agriculture: High-value crops (garlic, onions, tobacco)
  • Salt-making: Pangasinan ("place where salt is made")
  • Wine-making: Basi (fermented sugarcane wine)
  • Crafts: Inabel weaving, Burnay (clay pot) making

Region 2: Cagayan Valley

Geography: Located in northeastern Luzon; the largest region in Luzon. Includes the Cagayan River, the longest river in the Philippines.

"Tobacco Region of the Philippines"

Provinces: Batanes, Cagayan, Isabela, Nueva Vizcaya, Quirino

Livelihoods:

  • Farming: Top producer of yellow corn (Isabela), tobacco plantations
  • Logging: Sustainable forestry and wood processing
  • Fishing: Coastal and traditional Ivatan fishing

Cordillera Administrative Region (CAR)

Geography: Landlocked and mountainous; includes La Trinidad Valley - "Salad Bowl of the Philippines"

Provinces: Abra, Apayao, Benguet, Ifugao, Kalinga, Mountain Province

Livelihoods:

  • Highland Agriculture: Strawberries, lettuce, broccoli, "Baguio beans"
  • Mining: Gold and copper mining
  • Artisanship: Wood carving

Region 3: Central Luzon

Geography: The largest plain in the country.

"Rice Granary of the Philippines"

Provinces: Aurora, Bataan, Bulacan, Nueva Ecija, Pampanga, Tarlac, Zambales

Livelihoods:

  • Rice Production: Nueva Ecija is the leading producer
  • Aquaculture: Large-scale Bangus (Milkfish) and Tilapia farming
  • Industrial: Manufacturing hubs (Clark and Subic Freeport Zones)

National Capital Region (NCR)

Geography: The urbanized capital of the Philippines; mainly industrial and commercial.

Livelihoods:

  • Trade & Commerce: Banking, BPOs, corporate headquarters
  • Balut: Pateros (fertilized duck egg delicacy)
  • Shoes: Marikina - "Shoe Capital of the Philippines"
  • Others: Garments, electronics, food processing, fishing, salt making, furniture

Region IV-A: CALABARZON

Geography: Located in Southern Luzon; relatively mountainous with narrow plains.

Provinces: Cavite, Laguna, Batangas, Rizal, Quezon

Livelihoods:

  • Agriculture: Coconut (Quezon), Coffee Barako (Batangas)
  • Buntal hats: Quezon (handwoven from buri palm fibers)
  • Livestock: Batangas (cattle, poultry, beef, dairy)
  • Fishing: Coastal communities

Region IV-B: MIMAROPA

Geography: The "Island Neighbors" of Luzon on the south.

Provinces: Mindoro (Oriental & Occidental), Marinduque, Romblon, Palawan

Livelihoods:

  • Mining: Romblon - "Marble Capital"
  • Eco-Tourism & Fishing: Palawan (primary seafood source for Manila)

Region V: Bicol Region

Geography: Occupies the Bicol Peninsula in Southern Luzon. The terrain is generally mountainous and hilly.

"Abaca Region of the Philippines"

Provinces: Albay, Camarines Norte, Camarines Sur, Catanduanes, Masbate, Sorsogon

Livelihoods:

  • Agriculture: Pili nuts, coconut-based dishes (Bicol Express, pinangat)
  • Fishing: Abundant marine resources
  • Mining: Gold and copper (Masbate)
  • Handicrafts: Abaca fiber products, ceramics, pili nut delicacies

🏖️Visayas Regions

Region VI: Western Visayas

Geography: One of the island groups in Visayas, characterized by vast agricultural lands and rich fishing grounds.

Character: Third largest rice producer in the country

Provinces: Aklan, Antique, Capiz, Iloilo, Guimaras

Livelihoods:

  • Agriculture: Guimaras (world's sweetest mangoes), Iloilo (major rice producer)
  • Fishing: Capiz - "Seafood Capital of the Philippines"

Region VII: Central Visayas

Geography: Located in the central part of the Philippines between the islands of Luzon and Mindanao.

Provinces: Cebu, Bohol

Livelihoods:

  • Fishing: Rich coastal waters
  • Cattle Raising: Livestock and dairy production
  • Farming: Corn (primary crop), rice, coconut
  • Weaving: Sinamay (woven abaca fiber), piña fabric (pineapple fiber)
  • Mining: Limestone, gold

Region 8: Eastern Visayas

Geography: These islands have wide coastal areas and are mountainous.

Landmark: San Juanico Bridge - longest bridge in the Philippines (connects Leyte and Samar)

Provinces: Biliran, Leyte, Southern Leyte, Samar (Western, Northern, Eastern)

Livelihoods:

  • Home Industries: Banig (mat) weaving in Basey, Samar
  • Agriculture: Coconut and rice farming
  • Others: Fishing, logging, mining

Negros Island Region

Geography: A diverse region with rugged, mountainous areas and the high peak of Mt. Kanlaon. Marked by wide, fertile volcanic plains.

"Sugarbowl of the Philippines" - produces over half of the country's sugar

Provinces: Siquijor, Negros Occidental, Negros Oriental

Livelihoods:

  • Sugar Industry: Major sugar production
  • Energy: Geothermal power production (Valencia, Negros Oriental)

🌴Mindanao Regions

Region IX: Zamboanga Peninsula

Geography: Located in the southwestern part of the Philippines and connected to mainland Mindanao through a narrow strip of land.

Provinces: Zamboanga del Norte, Zamboanga del Sur, Zamboanga Sibugay

Livelihoods:

  • Sardine Industry: Zamboanga City - "Sardine Capital"
  • Cottage Industry: Weaving and brassware
  • Others: Farming, fishing

Region X: Northern Mindanao

Geography: Located in the north-central part of Mindanao. It is the gateway that links Mindanao to other parts of the Philippines.

Provinces: Bukidnon, Camiguin, Lanao del Norte, Misamis Occidental, Misamis Oriental

Livelihoods:

  • Plantations: Massive Pineapple (Del Monte) and Banana plantations (Bukidnon)
  • Industry: Heavy industries - steel and power (Iligan, Cagayan de Oro)
  • Others: Farming, fishing, mining, logging

Region XI: Davao Region

Geography: Composed of plateaus, plains, and mountains.

Provinces: Davao de Oro, Davao del Norte, Davao del Sur, Davao Oriental, Davao Occidental

Livelihoods:

  • Global Exports: Country's top exporter of Bananas, Durian, and Cacao
  • Others: Farming, fishing, mining

Region XII: SOCCSKSARGEN

Geography: Has a wide range of seashores, valleys, and mountain ranges. Known for its river systems which contribute to rich food sources and abundant water supply.

"Rice Bowl of Mindanao"

Provinces: South Cotabato, Cotabato, Sultan Kudarat, Sarangani

Livelihoods:

  • Tuna Industry: General Santos City - "Tuna Capital"
  • Ceramics: Growing ceramic and pottery industry
  • Agriculture: Rice and corn (main products)

Region XIII: CARAGA

Geography: Located in the northeastern part of Mindanao. Generally mountainous, with valleys along the coast.

Provinces: Agusan del Sur, Agusan del Norte, Surigao del Sur, Surigao del Norte, Dinagat Islands

Livelihoods:

  • Mining: Center of the Philippines' Nickel and Gold mining industry
  • Forestry: Wood processing and matchwood production (Agusan)
  • Others: Agriculture, fishing, logging

Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM)

Geography: Located in the southwestern part of the Philippines and comprises the Mindanao Island groups.

Provinces: Basilan, Lanao del Sur, Maguindanao, Sulu, Tawi-Tawi

Livelihoods:

  • Aquaculture: Pearl diving and seaweed farming (Agar-agar) in Tawi-Tawi
  • Crafts: Vinta making (Maguindanao), weaving
  • Others: Farming, fishing
